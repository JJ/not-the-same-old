Not the same old stuff

Distributed metaheuristics right now and the day after tomorrow

JJ Merelo / @jjmerelo

Most distributed evolutionary algorithms think the computing world is a little bit like that. Except the cat, that is. Regular, identical, static, always the same. Nothing changes and you can do stuff like parallel evolutionary algorithm of GPGPU or cluster computing or grid. Pretty straighforward, synchronous, predictable.
Of course, it's bit more like this. Nodes have different sizes, they are distributed unevenly and have different number of connections, and are as fragile as this spiderweb. It's also as numerous as the junctions or as the droplets in it, much more massive. And maybe you could take advantage of it.
This is the same old. Old tools, old systems, proprietary software
This is the environment you see today on the edge of the big data/cloud revolution. New languages, new paradigms, new methodologies for developing software. But what can we do about it?
The clouds exemplify very well what goes on in this new computing environment. First, because a part of it is literally called cloud. Second, because they are out there and there are lots of them, but they are predictable only in the short term and also ephemeral, here this moment, gone the next.
That's another characteristic of this new environment: it's available on tap. You pay cloud computing resources, CPU, storage, other more advanced applications like Map/Reduce, by the hour or minute. This means two things: you can have as much as you want, and second, this comes at a cost. Your algorithms will have to be scale and cost conscious, because you might be eventually wasting your grant just on computing resources.
In many cases, asynchrony is also the norm. First, because you can't expect virtual machines to start at exactly the same time or behave in exactly the same way, and they're spending while they are running. Second, because in the case of volunteer systems they might simply not be available. Even distributed computing can't work in lockstep, and there must be some way of permanently storing the algorithm state (or part of it) so that it can resume when resources are available.
The good thing is that you can gather a incredible amount of resources for free or a low price. A full month of a server running in Azure or Amazon can be a few dollars, a little bit more than a hosting. You can do a lot of things with the free tier of Platform as a Service or Infrastructure as a service. And, finally, if you go for volunteer computing the amount of resources can be unlimited in terms of computing nodes, although very limited in terms of time
You can obtain computing power aplenty this way (at a cost), but it comes at a cost: you have to change the methodology for developing software. Team/test/agile based using infrastructe-as-code/build/deployment tools. It's a small cost, since it makes your science, eventually, better
